Key-Phrase: Charter Schools

This graph displays the occurence of selected keywords within US media article titles from April 1, 2013 to present. Although the GDELT dataset extends to 2005, the SOURCEURL field was not included until April 1, 2013, and this field was used to find the keywords within the titles of the articles. The key-phrase 'Charter Schools' appeared more than any other phrase/keyword with 57,985 occurences. I was surprised that it was mentioned so much more than any other topic, so I decided to look at the number of mentions and average tone to further understand its representation within the media.

This graph displays the running averages, starting in 2014, of the average tone and number of mentions of articles mentioning charter schools in their titles. With the average tone of the articles on the left Y axis and the number of mentions on the right Y axis, this graph displays several interesting patterns. Firstly, as the running averags are calibrating, there are two inverse peaks and troughs between the number of mentions and average tone of the articles. The first of these inverse peaks occurs in early 2014, and the other in early 2015. Another interesting pattern is that since 2015 both the number of mentions of charter schools and the average tone steadily decline up until the present.

All three of these patterns suggest a slightly negative portrayal of charter schools in the US media considering that when the average tone peaked (i.e. positive articles involving charcter schools), the number of mentions of the articles reached a trough and when the average tone dipped the number of mentions reached its highest peak since 2014. However, I wanted to further investigate what is being said about Charter Schools, so I decided to look at the most mentioned articles from these three periods.

While these articles show the positive representation of charter schools within US media, since they correlate with a dip in the number of mentions they can be interpreted as being less important to US media readers. One article explains what the authors see as the advantages of charter schools, namely that smaller class sizes provide education that is more individualized than public schools, and that charter schools are held not only to the standards that public schools are held to, but also the goals set by their authorizers. Authorizers can be state agencies, school districts, or universities, and they have the ability to shut down charter schools that do not meet the terms of their contracts. This sentiment that charter schools have the ability to provide better education is mirrored in the positive articles from 2014 as well.
